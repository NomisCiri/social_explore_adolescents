Across all experimental treatments, adolescents and adults did not differ in points scored (two-sided t-test, t = -0.44, df = 140.16, p-value = 0.66). Regression analysis (Figure 3a) shows that the quality of the advisor significantly impacted performance. Both adolescents and adults performed the best in high quality advisor rounds, while performance declined in the remaining treatments (medium quality: b = -1.78, C.I.= [-2.06, -1.49], BF = 100; low quality: b = -1.57, C.I.= [-1.89, -1.24], BF = 100), indicating that worse social information led to fewer points gained. Finally, across all treatments there was no interaction effect between age group and advisor quality high_quality ado: [ = 0.01, C.I. = [-0.29, 0.32], BF = 1.14], (age groupmedium quality: b = 0.07, C.I. = [-0.28, 0.41], BF = 0.54; age grouplow: b = -0.04, C.I. = [-0.45, 0.36], BF = 1.33), suggesting that potential differences in social information use across treatments did not lead to differences in performance.

Next, we analysed differences in social information use. Across all experimental treatments, adolescents and adults copied advisors with similar frequencies. (two-sided t-test: t = -0.51, df = 167.52, p = 0.61, figure 2b). Interestingly, adolescents copied significantly more than adults in the rounds where the advisors were of high quality (adolescents mean = 9.68, s.d. =5.82; adults mean = 8.41, s.d. = 5.79; IR = 1.14; CI = [1.01, 1.3], BF = 51.17). In the rest of the experimental treatments there was no difference between adolescents and adults (medium quality*age_group: IR = 0.85; CI = [0.7, 1.02], BF = 22.94 ; low quality*age_group: IR = 0.95; CI = [0.78, 1.14], BF = 2.48), with both groups copying the medium quality advisor (adolescents mean = 6.48, s.d. = 5.38; adults mean = 6.34, s.d. = 6.34; IR = 0.71; CI = [0.61, 0.83], BF > 100), and the least when quality of social information was low (adolescents mean = , s.d. = ; adults mean = , s.d. = ; IR = 0.54; CI = [0.46, 0.62], BF > 100). Overall, these results indicate some adaptive social information use in both groups, and especially adolescents, who copied the most in treatments where advisor quality was the highest.

Finally, we looked at whether differences in social information quality affected the discovery of gems in rounds in which gems were present. Participants were most likely to find gems in high quality advisor rounds (adults: 80%, adolescents: 69%). Results of a multi-level logistic regression indicated that at the difference between groups was not significant (OR = 0.7, C.I.= [0.45, 1.07], BF = 18.7), suggesting that the observed differences in using high quality social information did not lead to differences in how many gems were found found. As expected, both groups were less likely to find gems in the remaining treatments (adults medium quality: 34%: OR = 0.21, C.I.= [0.13, 0.33], BF = 100; adults low quality: 36%, OR = 0.16, C.I.= [0.1, 0.26], BF = 100), indicating that worse social information led to less gems being discovered. In these treatment as well, there was no interaction effect between age group and advisor quality (adolescents medium: 32%, OR = 1.33, C.I. = [0.76, 2.37], BF = 0.19; adolescents low: 27%: OR = 1.01, C.I. = [0.56, 1.92], BF = 0.96).
